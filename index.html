Economic Contexts

Poverty, housing stress, and financial hardship shape the earliest experiences of many Australian children. Understanding economic disadvantage is fundamental to equitable early childhood practice.

Description

Economic disadvantage encompasses poverty, housing stress, food insecurity, and financial hardship. In Australia, child poverty is measured using the relative poverty line of 50% of median household income. Families experiencing economic hardship face compounding pressures including inadequate housing, limited access to nutritious food, restricted access to services, and chronic stress (Australian Council of Social Service [ACOSS], 2023).

Relevance to Early Childhood Education

The first five years of life are critical periods of brain development. Poverty creates "toxic stress" — prolonged activation of stress response systems — which disrupts neural architecture and has lasting developmental consequences (Shonkoff et al., 2012). Early childhood services are frequently the first point of contact for families experiencing hardship and are uniquely positioned to buffer developmental risk (Tayler, 2016).

Sociological Theories

Bronfenbrenner's (1979) Bioecological Model positions child development within nested environmental systems. Economic hardship operates across multiple levels: the microsystem (family stress, reduced play materials), the exosystem (parental unemployment, lack of community services), and the macrosystem (welfare policy, labour market conditions). (Bronfenbrenner & Morris, 2006)

Bourdieu's (1986) theory of capital highlights how economic capital intersects with social and cultural capital, creating cumulative disadvantage for children in low-income families who may have fewer books, less stimulating environments, and reduced access to enriching experiences (Bourdieu, 1986).

Strain Theory (Merton, 1938), as applied sociologically, suggests structural barriers prevent low-income families from achieving socially valued goals such as education and housing, increasing family stress and instability (ACOSS, 2023).

Diversity and Evolving Nature in Australia

Child poverty in Australia is not uniformly distributed. Single-parent families, families from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds, families in regional/remote areas, and First Nations families face significantly higher rates of economic disadvantage (ACOSS, 2023). The COVID-19 pandemic exacerbated housing stress, with rental affordability declining sharply in major cities (Australian Institute of Health and Welfare [AIHW], 2023a).

Developmental Impact

Children living in poverty are at significantly elevated risk of language and literacy delays, lower school readiness, and social-emotional difficulties (Dockett & Perry, 2014). Research using the Longitudinal Study of Australian Children (LSAC) found that children from the lowest income quintile were more than twice as likely to have developmental vulnerabilities on school entry compared to their more advantaged peers (Australian Institute of Family Studies [AIFS], 2022).

Housing instability — including frequent moves and overcrowding — disrupts attachment relationships, reduces access to stable early education placements, and impairs sleep, which is critical for early brain development (AIHW, 2023a).

Implications for Early Childhood Services

Services must recognise poverty-related stress in families without stigmatisation. Staff require training in trauma-informed practice to understand behaviours associated with chronic stress. Access barriers such as costs, transport, and service hours must be addressed to ensure equitable participation (Tayler, 2016).

Key Policies and Initiatives

  • Child Care Subsidy (CCS): Introduced under the Family Assistance Legislation Amendment (Child Care Subsidy) Act 2017, the CCS subsidises childcare costs on an income-tested basis. From July 2023, the subsidy increased to 90% for eligible families earning under $80,000 per year (Department of Education, 2023).
  • National Housing and Homelessness Agreement (NHHA): A joint Commonwealth-State agreement funding homelessness services and social housing (AIHW, 2023a).
  • Closing the Gap (Priority Reform 3): Addresses socioeconomic disadvantage for First Nations communities, including targets for school readiness and early childhood education attendance (Australian Government, 2023).
  • NSW Early Childhood Education Strategy 2021–2026: Focuses on increasing preschool participation among disadvantaged children, including fee subsidies for vulnerable families (NSW Department of Education, 2021).
  • Start Strong Programme (NSW): Provides funding to community preschools to reduce fees for eligible families (NSW Department of Education, 2021).

Policy Evaluation

The CCS has improved access to early childhood education for middle-income families, but families in the deepest poverty — including those receiving welfare payments — often remain unable to afford gap fees (ACOSS, 2023). The Early Years Learning Framework v2.0 (EYLF; Department of Education, 2022) embeds equity and inclusion as foundational principles, directing practitioners to actively counter disadvantage.

  • 1. Trauma-Informed Practice Adopt a whole-service trauma-informed approach, recognising that poverty creates chronic stress. This includes building predictable routines, warm and responsive caregiving, and avoiding punitive responses to dysregulated behaviour (Bath, 2015; Brunzell et al., 2016).
  • 2. Strengths-Based Family Engagement Use strengths-based communication frameworks (e.g., motivational interviewing) to engage families in collaborative goal-setting, avoiding deficit framing that pathologises poverty (Dockett & Perry, 2014).
  • 3. Resource-Rich Learning Environments Ensure the early childhood environment is rich with quality books, play materials, and nutritious meals/snacks, compensating for resource-limited home environments (Shonkoff et al., 2012).
  • 4. Referral Pathways and Case Coordination Build clear referral pathways to financial counselling, housing support, food banks, and family support services. Maintain an up-to-date community resource directory (Tayler, 2016).
  • 5. Flexible Enrolment and Fee Relief Proactively apply for fee assistance, subsidies, and flexible enrolment arrangements. Communicate access options in plain language and in home languages to reduce participation barriers (Department of Education, 2022; NSW Department of Education, 2021).
  • 1. Mission Australia Provides housing support, homelessness services, and financial counselling. ECE services can refer families to local Mission Australia offices for emergency relief and case management (Mission Australia, 2024).
  • 2. Anglicare Australia Offers emergency food relief, financial counselling, and family support programs. Collaboration enables integrated service delivery for vulnerable families (Anglicare Australia, 2024).
  • 3. Financial Counsellors (National Debt Helpline) Accredited financial counsellors help families navigate debt and budgeting. ECE teachers can provide families with referral information and warm handover support (Financial Counselling Australia, 2024).
  • 4. Foodbank Australia Operates food relief networks and school breakfast programs. ECE services can partner with local Foodbank to provide on-site food relief for families (Foodbank Australia, 2023).
  • 5. Centrelink / Services Australia Administers family assistance payments, rent assistance, and welfare support. ECE staff can support families to navigate entitlements and connect with social workers (Services Australia, 2024).

Social Contexts

Social and geographical isolation, family separation, divorce, and out-of-home care place children at the intersections of vulnerability and resilience.

Description

Social contexts of disadvantage include social isolation (lack of meaningful connection), geographical isolation (remote or rural residence), family breakdown, divorce/separation, and out-of-home care (OOHC). These contexts disrupt children's sense of security, belonging, and connection — foundational elements of healthy development (Australian Institute of Family Studies [AIFS], 2022).

Relevance to Early Childhood Education

Australia's geographically vast landscape means that approximately 28% of children under five live in outer regional, remote, or very remote areas with limited access to early childhood services (AIHW, 2023b). Children in OOHC have typically experienced abuse, neglect, or family violence, making early childhood settings critical stabilising environments. Divorce and separation — affecting approximately 50,000 Australian children annually — disrupts attachment and routine (ABS, 2022).

Sociological Theories

Attachment Theory (Bowlby, 1969; Ainsworth, 1978) provides the foundational lens: children require consistent, responsive caregiving to develop a secure base from which to explore. Children experiencing family breakdown or OOHC frequently present with insecure or disorganised attachment styles, requiring attuned, patient caregiving relationships in early childhood settings (Bowlby, 1988; Ainsworth et al., 1978).

Social Capital Theory (Putnam, 2000) highlights how social isolation erodes community bonds (bridging and bonding capital), reducing families' access to informal support networks that buffer adversity. Early childhood services become critical sites for rebuilding social capital for isolated families (Putnam, 2000).

Diversity and Evolving Nature

Australia's diverse family structures — including sole-parent families, blended families, grandparent-led families, and same-sex parent families — mean that "family breakdown" cannot be framed simplistically. Increasing rates of grandparent carers (now 30,000+ in formal kinship care in Australia) reflect changing family dynamics requiring culturally responsive practice (AIHW, 2023c).

Children in OOHC demonstrate higher rates of developmental delay, emotional and behavioural difficulties, and school disengagement compared to the general population (Fernandez, 2014). The disruption of placement changes further compounds these risks by severing attachment relationships with carers and educators.

Geographical isolation limits access to specialist services (speech pathology, psychology, paediatrics), reduces social learning opportunities with peers, and increases carer stress and parental loneliness — all of which negatively impact children's outcomes (AIHW, 2023b).

Children experiencing parental separation may exhibit increased anxiety, regressive behaviour, sleep difficulties, and withdrawal. Research indicates that parental conflict — not separation per se — is the key predictor of poor child outcomes, emphasising the importance of cooperative co-parenting support (Amato, 2014).

  • Child Protection Legislation (State/Territory): Each state and territory has child protection legislation (e.g., Children and Young Persons (Care and Protection) Act 1998 NSW) that governs OOHC placement, review, and case planning, with restoration to family as the primary goal (NSW Government, 2023).
  • National Framework for Protecting Australia's Children 2021–2031: Sets a national vision for child safety, with priority areas including supporting families to stay together and early intervention (Department of Social Services, 2021).
  • NDIS — Early Childhood Approach: Provides early intervention supports for children under 9 with developmental delay or disability, including those in OOHC (National Disability Insurance Agency, 2023).
  • Connected Beginnings (remote communities): Jointly funded by the Commonwealth to integrate early childhood, family support, and health services in remote Indigenous communities (Department of Education, 2023).
  • Family Law Act 1975: Governs arrangements for children following parental separation, emphasising the best interests of the child and ongoing relationships with both parents (Australian Government, 1975).
  • 1. Key Person Approach Assign each child a consistent key educator who builds a warm, predictable relationship. For children in OOHC or from disrupted families, this attachment figure provides a vital "secure base" within the service (Page, 2011).
  • 2. Inclusive Family Documentation Use flexible definitions of "family" in all documentation, displays, and activities. Invite all family structures — grandparent carers, foster carers, sole parents — to participate in service life without tokenism (Department of Education, 2022).
  • 3. Social-Emotional Learning (SEL) Curriculum Embed evidence-based SEL programs (e.g., Kimochis, Zippy's Friends) that explicitly develop emotional literacy, friendship skills, and resilience for socially isolated or separated children (Durlak et al., 2011).
  • 4. Telehealth and Technology-Supported Learning For geographically isolated families, facilitate access to telehealth speech pathology, psychology, and allied health. Use digital communication tools to maintain family engagement (AIHW, 2023b).
  • 5. Trauma-Informed Transitions For children moving between placements or following family separation, develop individualised transition plans with overlap periods, comfort objects from home, and dedicated communication between carers and educators (Brunzell et al., 2016).
  • 1. CREATE Foundation Australia's peak body representing children and young people in OOHC. Provides training resources for educators working with children in care (CREATE Foundation, 2024).
  • 2. Relationships Australia Provides family counselling, mediation, and co-parenting support following separation. ECE services can refer families experiencing parental conflict (Relationships Australia, 2024).
  • 3. Beyond Blue (Family Support Programs) Mental health support and resources for isolated families and carers experiencing stress and loneliness (Beyond Blue, 2024).
  • 4. UnitingCare Australia Delivers Out of Home Care, kinship support, and family restoration programs across multiple states (UnitingCare Australia, 2024).
  • 5. Allied Health — Speech Pathology Australia / Psychology Specialist support for developmental delays common in isolated children and those in OOHC. Educators collaborate via referrals and joint planning (Speech Pathology Australia, 2024).

Cultural & Diversity Contexts

Australia's cultural diversity — including First Nations families and immigrant and refugee communities — enriches early childhood settings while presenting distinct responsibilities for culturally responsive practice.

Description

Cultural and diversity contexts encompass First Nations Australian families, immigrant families, refugee and asylum-seeker families, and multilingual families. Australia is one of the world's most culturally diverse nations: approximately 30% of residents were born overseas, and over 300 languages are spoken (ABS, 2021). First Nations Australians — representing over 250 distinct language groups — have the world's oldest living cultures and distinct rights to cultural continuity and self-determination (Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies [AIATSIS], 2022).

Relevance to Early Childhood Education

Cultural identity is a foundational dimension of children's wellbeing and sense of self. The EYLF v2.0 (Department of Education, 2022) recognises cultural competence and Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander perspectives as non-negotiable elements of quality practice. Refugee and newly arrived children may have experienced significant trauma, disruption, and language discontinuity, requiring specialist support (Block et al., 2014).

Sociological Theories

Cultural-Historical Activity Theory (Vygotsky, 1978) emphasises that children's learning and development are inseparable from cultural tools, language, and social interaction. When children's home cultures are excluded from early childhood settings, their cognitive and social development is constrained (Vygotsky, 1978).

Postcolonial Theory (Said, 1978; Moreton-Robinson, 2004) reveals how dominant Western frameworks in early childhood education can marginalise First Nations knowledge systems and perpetuate deficit views of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander families. Anti-bias, decolonising pedagogy is essential to redressing this (Moreton-Robinson, 2004).

Acculturation Theory (Berry, 1997) describes the psychological and social processes refugee and immigrant families navigate in adapting to a new culture. Integration — maintaining home culture while engaging with the new — is associated with the best outcomes for children (Berry, 1997).

Diversity and Evolving Nature

Australia's refugee intake has increased following global crises (Syria, Afghanistan, Myanmar, South Sudan). The 2021 Census recorded over 300 ancestries and a growing proportion of children entering early childhood education with English as an Additional Language or Dialect (EAL/D) (ABS, 2021).

First Nations children face significantly higher rates of developmental vulnerability on the Australian Early Development Census (AEDC): in 2021, 43% of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children were developmentally vulnerable in one or more domains, compared to 21% of non-Indigenous children (Australian Early Development Census [AEDC], 2022). These disparities reflect historical and ongoing impacts of colonisation, not inherent deficits.

Refugee children frequently present with trauma responses, interrupted schooling, and language barriers. However, research consistently demonstrates extraordinary resilience when children receive culturally affirming, trauma-sensitive support (Block et al., 2014).

Language discontinuity — where children's home language is not supported in early education — disrupts bilingual development, cultural identity, and parental engagement (Cummins, 2001).

  • Closing the Gap (2020–2030): Co-designed with Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities, with specific targets for early childhood education attendance and developmental outcomes (Australian Government, 2023).
  • National Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Early Childhood Strategy: Guides investment in culturally safe, community-controlled early childhood education services for First Nations children (Department of Education, 2021).
  • Multicultural Access and Equity Policy (2018): Commonwealth framework requiring government services to be accessible and responsive to people from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds (Department of Home Affairs, 2018).
  • National Quality Standard (NQS) — Quality Area 6: Requires services to actively partner with families and communities, including culturally diverse and First Nations families (Australian Children's Education and Care Quality Authority [ACECQA], 2023).
  • EAL/D Funding (State-based): States such as NSW and Victoria provide additional funding for children with English as an Additional Language or Dialect in early childhood settings (NSW Department of Education, 2023).
  • 1. Anti-Bias Curriculum Implement Derman-Sparks and Edwards' (2010) anti-bias curriculum framework, which actively addresses prejudice, power, and cultural identity in early learning environments. Ensure all children see themselves reflected in books, resources, and displays (Derman-Sparks & Edwards, 2010).
  • 2. Home Language Support Value and incorporate children's home languages through bilingual resources, multilingual signage, songs in home languages, and engaging community language speakers. This sustains cultural identity and supports cognitive development (Cummins, 2001).
  • 3. Genuine Community Consultation (First Nations) Engage with local Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities, Elders, and Land Councils to ensure cultural knowledge and practices are authentically incorporated — not tokenistically (AIATSIS, 2022).
  • 4. Culturally Safe Trauma-Informed Practice (Refugee Families) Use the Refugee Health Screener and Cultural Formulation Interview as frameworks. Build refugee family trust through consistent, warm contact; avoid requiring disclosure of trauma history (Block et al., 2014).
  • 5. Family Cultural Evenings and Celebrations Organise inclusive family events that invite diverse cultural contributions. Document and share children's cultural funds of knowledge as curriculum content (González et al., 2005).
  • 1. Local Aboriginal Land Council / Community-Controlled Organisations Provide culturally authentic connections, Acknowledgement of Country support, and cultural knowledge sharing for early childhood services (AIATSIS, 2022).
  • 2. Settlement Services International (SSI) Australia's largest settlement and refugee services organisation. Provides cultural liaison, interpreter services, and family support for newly arrived communities (SSI, 2024).
  • 3. Multicultural NSW / State Equivalents Government bodies providing interpreter services, anti-discrimination resources, and multicultural education support for early childhood services (Multicultural NSW, 2024).
  • 4. STARTTS (NSW Service for the Treatment and Rehabilitation of Torture and Trauma Survivors) Specialist trauma rehabilitation for refugee families; provides professional consultation and training for educators (STARTTS, 2024).
  • 5. EAL/D Specialist Teachers State education departments employ EAL/D specialists who can provide assessment, programming support, and professional learning for early childhood educators (NSW Department of Education, 2023).

Health & Wellbeing Contexts

Parental mental illness, substance use, trauma, grief, and abuse profoundly shape children's earliest experiences. Early childhood services play a critical role in recognising risk and building protective relationships.

Description

Health and wellbeing contexts include parental mental illness (depression, anxiety, psychosis), parental substance misuse (alcohol, drugs), childhood trauma (physical, sexual, emotional abuse, neglect), grief and loss, and exposure to domestic and family violence (AIHW, 2022a). These contexts often co-occur and create cumulative adversity — sometimes called "adverse childhood experiences" (ACEs) — with dose-response relationships to poor health and developmental outcomes (Felitti et al., 1998).

Relevance to Early Childhood Education

Approximately 1 in 5 Australian adults experience mental illness in any given year, with parental mental illness being one of the strongest predictors of child developmental difficulty (AIHW, 2022a). The early childhood period is when neurobiological foundations for stress regulation, social-emotional functioning, and learning are established. Trauma experienced in this period has the greatest developmental impact, yet also the greatest capacity for recovery with appropriate support (Shonkoff et al., 2012; National Scientific Council on the Developing Child, 2020).

Sociological Theories

Trauma-Informed Care Framework (Harris & Fallot, 2001) recognises that many challenging behaviours in children and families are trauma responses rather than pathology, and that services must be built on principles of safety, trustworthiness, choice, collaboration, and empowerment (Harris & Fallot, 2001).

ACEs (Adverse Childhood Experiences) Framework (Felitti et al., 1998): Landmark research demonstrating that cumulative childhood adversity has dose-response relationships with adult physical and mental health outcomes. Even two ACEs significantly increase risk for poor outcomes (Felitti et al., 1998).

Resilience Theory (Masten, 2001): Identifies protective factors — including stable, caring adult relationships, sense of agency, and community belonging — that buffer the impact of adversity. This "ordinary magic" of resilience is cultivated in quality early childhood settings (Masten, 2001).

Diversity and Evolving Nature

Mental health impacts are not uniformly distributed: First Nations Australians experience disproportionately higher rates of mental health challenges due to ongoing intergenerational trauma from colonisation, the Stolen Generations, and systemic racism (AIHW, 2022a). Perinatal mental illness — depression and anxiety during pregnancy and the first postnatal year — affects approximately 1 in 5 women and 1 in 10 men in Australia (Perinatal Anxiety & Depression Australia [PANDA], 2023).

Parental depression, particularly perinatal depression, impairs sensitive caregiving during the critical period of attachment formation (birth to 2 years). This creates elevated risk of insecure attachment, which in turn predicts difficulties with social competence, emotional regulation, and academic engagement (Murray et al., 2011).

Children exposed to parental substance misuse face heightened risks of neglect, inconsistent caregiving, exposure to unsafe environments, and foetal alcohol spectrum disorder (FASD) — Australia's leading preventable developmental disability (NOFASD Australia, 2023).

Research on childhood trauma demonstrates that unresolved trauma disrupts neurobiological stress response systems (the HPA axis), producing hypervigilance, emotional dysregulation, and learning difficulties that, without intervention, persist across the lifespan (van der Kolk, 2014).

  • National Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Plan (2022–2031): Commits to a whole-of-government approach including perinatal mental health services, early intervention, and integrated care (Australian Government, 2022).
  • National Drug Strategy (2017–2026): Harm minimisation framework addressing alcohol and other drug use, including parental substance misuse and impacts on children (Australian Government, 2017).
  • Child Safe Standards (National): Mandatory standards across child-related organisations to prevent and respond to child abuse and neglect, embedded in state/territory child protection legislation (ACECQA, 2023).
  • Mandatory Reporting Legislation: All states and territories require early childhood educators to report suspected child abuse and neglect to statutory child protection authorities (AIHW, 2023c).
  • Perinatal Mental Health Funding (Better Access): Medicare-funded psychology sessions through Better Access and specific perinatal mental health services under the MBS (Department of Health and Aged Care, 2023).
  • 1. Trauma-Informed Practice (TIP) Implement ARTIC (Assessment and Review — Trauma-Informed Care) frameworks. Create safe, predictable environments with regulated adults who co-regulate children experiencing dysregulation (Brunzell et al., 2016; Bath, 2015).
  • 2. Observe, Report, and Support (Mandatory Reporting) Ensure all educators are trained in child protection obligations. Maintain detailed, objective observations. Prioritise child safety through timely reporting to child protection authorities while maintaining supportive family relationships where safe to do so (ACECQA, 2023).
  • 3. Socio-Emotional Learning Programs Use evidence-based programs such as Seasons for Growth (grief and loss), MindMatters (mental health literacy), and Kimochis (emotional literacy) to build children's emotional vocabulary and self-regulation (Durlak et al., 2011).
  • 4. Strength-Based Parenting Support Provide non-judgemental, strengths-based family support through regular family meetings, parenting information sessions, and warm referrals to mental health and family services. Avoid shaming language around parental mental illness or substance use (Masten, 2001).
  • 5. Sensory-Regulation and Nature-Based Environments Design environments with sensory regulation in mind — quiet spaces, soft lighting, predictable sensory input — and incorporate nature play, which has evidence-based benefits for stress reduction and emotional regulation in trauma-affected children (van der Kolk, 2014; Dankiw et al., 2020).
  • 1. PANDA (Perinatal Anxiety and Depression Australia) National helpline and resources for perinatal mental illness. ECE services can provide referral information and posters in family spaces (PANDA, 2023).
  • 2. Child & Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS) State-based specialist services providing assessment, diagnosis, and therapy for children with complex mental health needs. Educators collaborate through referral and shared care plans (AIHW, 2022a).
  • 3. Family Drug Support Australia Supports families affected by a loved one's substance misuse, including carers of young children. Provides educator training and family referrals (Family Drug Support Australia, 2024).
  • 4. Lifeline Australia 24-hour crisis support and suicide prevention services. ECE services display Lifeline contact information and can provide immediate referrals for families in acute distress (Lifeline, 2024).
  • 5. Social Workers (Health and Community Services) Hospital-based and community social workers provide case management, family support, and child protection coordination. Educators partner with social workers in integrated care planning (Australian Association of Social Workers [AASW], 2024).

Crisis & Emergency Contexts

Domestic and family violence, natural disasters, climate events, and forced displacement are acute crises that disrupt children's safety, security, and sense of the world as a predictable place.

Description

Crisis and emergency contexts include domestic and family violence (DFV), natural disasters (bushfires, floods, cyclones), climate-related displacement, and forced displacement from conflict. Australia experiences some of the world's most extreme natural hazard events, and DFV is the leading cause of homelessness for women and children in Australia (Australian Institute of Criminology [AIC], 2023).

Relevance to Early Childhood Education

Young children are disproportionately vulnerable in crises: they have the least developed stress regulation capacities, are entirely dependent on caregivers (who may themselves be traumatised), and lack the cognitive resources to comprehend and contextualise dangerous events (Osofsky, 2011). ECE services in crisis-affected communities become critical "anchor institutions" providing continuity, safety, and routine amid chaos (Dockett & Perry, 2014).

Sociological Theories

Ecological Systems Theory (Bronfenbrenner, 1979) reveals how crises cascade across systems: a bushfire damages the macrosystem (community infrastructure), exosystem (employment, services), and microsystem (family stress, displacement) simultaneously. Recovery requires intervention at all system levels (Bronfenbrenner & Morris, 2006).

Community Resilience Theory (Norris et al., 2008) identifies community adaptive capacities — economic development, social capital, information infrastructure, and community competence — as predictors of post-disaster recovery and child wellbeing (Norris et al., 2008).

Gender-Based Violence Theory: DFV is understood as a gendered crime rooted in power and control, not individual pathology. The Duluth Model (Pence & Paymar, 1993) and coercive control frameworks reveal the systematic nature of abuse experienced by women and children (Pence & Paymar, 1993).

Diversity and Evolving Nature

Climate change is intensifying the frequency and severity of natural disasters in Australia: the 2019–20 Black Summer bushfires, the 2022 eastern Australia floods, and projections of more frequent extreme heat events disproportionately affect rural, remote, and low-income communities (Climate Council, 2023). First Nations communities face compounding vulnerabilities in climate crises due to remoteness, economic disadvantage, and reduced service access.

Children exposed to DFV demonstrate significantly elevated rates of PTSD, anxiety, depression, attachment difficulties, and developmental regression (Holt et al., 2008). Witnessing DFV — even without direct physical harm — activates the same neurobiological stress pathways as direct abuse. The 2019–20 Royal Commission into Violence, Abuse, Neglect and Exploitation of People with Disability confirmed that children with disabilities face heightened DFV exposure (Royal Commission, 2023).

Natural disaster exposure in early childhood is associated with increases in behavioural problems, separation anxiety, sleep disturbances, regressive behaviour (bed-wetting, clingy behaviour), and post-traumatic stress symptoms (Osofsky, 2011). Recovery is strongly mediated by caregiver mental health: parental distress following disaster is the strongest predictor of child post-disaster PTSD (Norris et al., 2008).

  • National Plan to End Violence Against Women and Children 2022–2032: Ten-year framework with First Action Plan 2023–2027, prioritising prevention, early intervention, and support for children experiencing DFV (Department of Social Services, 2022).
  • National Disaster Risk Reduction Framework (2018): Guides disaster risk reduction policy including community resilience building and early childhood service continuity planning (Australian Government, 2018).
  • NSW Domestic Violence Disclosure Scheme (Clare's Law): Enables people to request information about a partner's history of violence to support safety planning (NSW Government, 2023).
  • Emergency Services Childcare Declaration (COVID/Disaster Provision): Ensures continuity of approved childcare services during declared emergencies, including disaster events (Department of Education, 2023).
  • Ending Family Violence: Victoria's Plan for Change: World-first, evidence-based family violence reform plan, including specific measures for early childhood services to identify and respond to DFV (Victorian Government, 2022).
  • 1. Safety Planning and DFV Protocols Develop and rehearse clear DFV response protocols, including risk identification frameworks (e.g., Children Experiencing Domestic Violence: A Framework for Action). Train all staff in recognising indicators of DFV and safe referral pathways to 1800RESPECT and local DFV services (AIC, 2023).
  • 2. Emergency and Disaster Preparedness Planning Develop and practice Family Emergency Plans with children as part of the curriculum. Create age-appropriate learning about disaster preparedness through play. Maintain updated service continuity plans aligned with state emergency management frameworks (Dockett & Perry, 2014).
  • 3. Restore Safety and Routine Post-Crisis Following crisis events, prioritise re-establishing familiar routines, predictable environments, and calm, regulated adult presence. Avoid pushing children to "talk through" traumatic events — instead, provide play-based opportunities for narrative and expression (van der Kolk, 2014; Osofsky, 2011).
  • 4. Expressive Arts and Play Therapy Approaches Incorporate art, music, dramatic play, sandplay, and narrative play as vehicles for children to process crisis experiences without requiring verbal disclosure. Partner with play therapists for children presenting with acute trauma responses (Malchiodi, 2011).
  • 5. Climate Literacy and Ecological Resilience Building Embed age-appropriate climate literacy into the curriculum — fostering children's sense of agency, environmental connection, and community responsibility, while avoiding eco-anxiety. Garden-based, nature-play and sustainability projects promote resilience (Climate Council, 2023; Dankiw et al., 2020).
  • 1. 1800RESPECT National sexual assault, domestic and family violence counselling service. ECE services display information and can make direct referrals for affected families (1800RESPECT, 2024).
  • 2. Red Cross Australia Provides emergency relief, community recovery programs, and disaster preparedness training for communities and services (Australian Red Cross, 2024).
  • 3. White Ribbon Australia Primary prevention organisation working with communities and organisations to prevent men's violence against women. Provides educator training and workplace accreditation (White Ribbon Australia, 2024).
  • 4. State Emergency Service (SES) Delivers community disaster education, first aid training, and emergency preparedness workshops that ECE services can access to develop disaster plans and educate children and families (NSW SES, 2024).
  • 5. Play Therapists / Trauma-Specialist Psychologists Registered play therapists and child psychologists with trauma specialisation provide direct therapeutic support for children exposed to crisis events. ECE educators collaborate through referral, shared planning, and joint observation (Australian Association of Play Therapy [AAPT], 2024).
